A Forward Deployed Engineer isn't just a software engineer who talks to customers. It's a different operating model — you don't get to hand off the context when you sit down to write code.

The Philosophy

Build for the problem, not the demo.

Most software fails in deployment — not because it was written wrong, but because it was written for the wrong thing. The gap between what a customer describes and what they actually need is where most projects quietly die.

My approach starts before the first line of code. Understand the environment. Map the actual workflow — not the documented one. Identify the constraint that everyone works around but no one talks about. Then build directly at that point.

The result is software that gets adopted because it fits, not because it was forced.

Process

How I Work

Four phases. Each one grounded in real constraints, not ideal conditions.

01

Discovery

Understand the environment before proposing anything. This means talking to the people who actually do the work, not just the people who manage it. I map the real workflow — the undocumented one — and identify where friction lives.

Process mapping Stakeholder interviews Data landscape audit
02

Architecture

Design for the real constraints, not the ideal ones. What's the network environment? What does the auth infrastructure look like? What's the data model that already exists? I build on top of what's there, not against it.

Schema design API contracts Auth strategy Infra planning
03

Execution

Build fast, build right, iterate with the customer in the loop. Demo early so direction can correct before it's expensive to change. Ship incrementally so each deployment builds confidence rather than accumulating risk.

Iterative delivery Stakeholder demos CI/CD pipelines E2E test coverage
04

Handoff

A system that only works while I'm around isn't a solution. Handoff means documentation that a new engineer can act on, observability that tells operators when something's wrong before a user does, and a knowledge transfer that actually transfers knowledge.

Technical documentation Observability stack Operational playbook
Value

What Changes When I Join

Framed from the org's perspective.

→

Your team ships internal tooling faster.

I can scope, architect, and build a production-grade internal tool in weeks. Not quarters. That means faster feedback loops, earlier adoption, and less time spent on internal tools that never get used.

→

Your stakeholders get answers without opening a ticket.

Self-service dashboards, automated reports, and well-structured APIs mean your data team stops being a request queue. Stakeholders pull what they need. The bottleneck disappears.

→

Your systems are observable from day one.

I don't ship without monitoring. Prometheus, Grafana, health endpoints, and structured logging mean you know what's happening in your infrastructure before something breaks — not after.

→

Your technical debt gets documented, not inherited.

Legacy systems I touch get documented. Every decision gets a rationale. The codebase I leave behind is something a new engineer can navigate on their first week — not in their third month.

Fit

Large orgs with complex network segmentation, strict auth requirements, legacy infrastructure, and multiple stakeholder groups. I've operated in both.

Ambiguous Scope

When the problem is clear but the solution isn't. I'm comfortable driving from "we need something better" to a scoped, shipped product.

Greenfield in Legacy

Building new systems alongside old ones — respecting what works, modernizing what doesn't, and never breaking what people depend on today.

Cross-Functional Teams

Translating between engineering, operations, and executive stakeholders. Making technical tradeoffs legible to non-technical decision-makers.

Tight Timelines

When weeks matter more than quarters. I scope aggressively, cut what doesn't serve the outcome, and ship something real — then iterate.

The Differentiator

The Cross-Functional Edge

Most engineers can build a system. Fewer can run a DMAIC, document a process to Six Sigma standards, and deploy a FastAPI backend in the same week.

A Six Sigma Black Belt isn't a credential to list on a resume — it's a discipline for thinking about process. It means approaching a broken workflow the same way you'd approach a broken query: measure, identify root cause, test a fix, validate.

The result is automation that addresses root causes — not automation built on top of a process that shouldn't exist in the first place.
