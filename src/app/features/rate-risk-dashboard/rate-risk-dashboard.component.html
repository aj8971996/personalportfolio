Data sources

All yield data comes from the Federal Reserve's FRED database (H.15 Selected Interest Rates — daily, Treasury constant maturity, not seasonally adjusted). It's public and free.

  • 3-Month [DGS3MO] — 1982-01-04 to 2024-12-31 (11,216 trading days)
  • 2-Year [DGS2] — 1976-06-01 to 2024-12-31 (12,675 trading days)
  • 5-Year [DGS5] — 1962-01-02 to 2024-12-31 (16,435 trading days)
  • 10-Year [DGS10] — 1962-01-02 to 2024-12-31 (16,435 trading days)
  • 30-Year [DGS30] — 1977-02-15 to 2024-12-31, with a gap from 2002-02-01 to 2006-02-08 when Treasury stopped issuing 30Y bonds (11,440 trading days total; gap treated as missing, not filled)

Why different maturities show different "impossible" counts

The counter picks the highest sigma level (e.g. 8×) where real events exist, then compares that count to what the normal model predicts. Two things explain why 10Y and 30Y differ:

  • More data = more chances. 10Y and 5Y go back to 1962 (16,435 days). 30Y starts in 1977 and has a 4-year gap, leaving only 11,440 days. Rare events are rare — more data means more exposure to catching them.
  • Different typical swings. 30Y bonds are more volatile (σ ≈ 15 bps vs 13 bps for 10Y), so its "8×" threshold is a larger absolute move. A higher bar means fewer events qualify.

Both are correct. The observation count (shown in the chips below the headline) is the key context.

Key terms, plain English

σ (sigma / standard deviation)
The typical size of a daily rate move, computed from the full history. A "3σ day" means the move was 3 times that typical size.
Tail weight (excess kurtosis)
A score for how heavy the extreme ends of the distribution are. A true normal distribution scores 0. Treasury data typically scores 4–8, meaning extreme moves happen far more often than the model expects.
Value-at-Risk (VaR)
The loss level a normal model says you shouldn't exceed more than X% of trading days. A 99% 1-day VaR means the model expects a breach about 2–3 times per year. With fat tails, the real breach rate is higher.
The tail shape chart
Sorts all daily moves from smallest to largest, then plots each one against where a normal distribution would expect it to fall. Points hugging the diagonal = normal. Points bending away at the corners = heavier tails than the model assumes.

Honest caveats

Fat tails in financial returns are a known, documented fact — not a bug in this analysis. Real daily yield changes have consistently scored excess kurtosis between 4 and 8 across all Treasury maturities. That means a VaR limit sized on the normal model will be breached more often than it should. This dashboard measures that gap; it doesn't claim to predict when the next extreme day will occur.

In a production environment, this same analysis connects to a TMS or risk system instead of FRED. The method — fit a distribution, compute VaR, measure actual vs. expected tail breaches — is identical.
